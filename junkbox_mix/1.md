Here is the shortlist I would use for a small, solar-throttleable setup, ranked by the best mix of simplicity, impurity tolerance, and likely product margin. The common pattern is: waste feed, simple DC cell, and a product that is already a commodity or an industrial intermediate. 

1. Copper electrowinning from acidic leachates or e-waste leachate.
This is the strongest practical option. Electrowinning is described as a simple, chemical-free bath process, and recent work shows high-purity copper can be directly electrowon from iron-rich e-waste leachate without prior iron removal, reaching about 99.9% Cu purity under optimized low-current conditions. That makes it a good fit for dirty feeds and for solar operation, because you can simply reduce current when power is low. 


2. Precious-metal recovery from e-waste leachates by selective electrodeposition.
If your feed actually contains Au, Ag, Pd, or high-grade Cu, this has the best upside per liter of solution. Reviews note electrowinning is simple and chemical-free, and is used for gold, silver, copper, cadmium, and zinc recovery; a critical review also reports selective copper recovery from mixed leachates containing Pb, Sn, and Al impurities. The economics can be excellent, but only if the upstream leach chemistry is managed well enough to avoid contamination of the cathode product. 


3. Crude glycerol electro-oxidation to formate.
This is one of the best “dirty feed to sellable product” routes outside metals. Crude glycerol is typically a mixture of glycerol, methanol, water, sodium hydroxide, and other impurities, and a 2025 flow-electrolyzer study showed that impurity effects can be managed with dynamic operation while still producing formate stably for hours. That is a strong match for intermittent solar power and low-cost hardware. 


4. Nitrate-to-ammonia electroreduction from wastewater.
This is attractive when the feed is nitrate-rich wastewater rather than a clean reagent stream. A 2026 review says electrochemical nitrate reduction simultaneously removes nitrate and makes ammonia, and describes it as cost-effective, environmentally benign, and energy efficient; a 2025 solar-driven system also demonstrated high-rate ammonia production from wastewater in a photovoltaic-electrocatalytic setup. The product is marketable, but catalyst selectivity and analytical control are more demanding than for copper or struvite. 


5. Electrochemical struvite recovery for fertilizer.
This is the simplest nutrient-recovery path that still produces a sellable product. Reviews report that Mg sacrificial-anode systems can reduce the need for added chemicals or pH adjustment and can achieve over 90% phosphate removal with high-purity struvite, while a prospective cost/LCA study found the electrochemical sequence minimized chemical use and had competitive energy consumption. The product is lower-value than copper or precious metals, but the setup is comparatively forgiving. 


6. CO₂ electrolysis to CO or formate.
Technically interesting, but I would rank it last for your criteria. A major 2023 perspective notes that industrial deployment is not yet imminent and that impurity-tolerant systems still need development; CO₂ electrolyzers have shown intermittent operation under solar-like power input, but carbonate formation, crossover, and separation remain major issues. Good science, weaker small-scale economics. 



The cleanest practical winners are copper electrowinning, precious-metal recovery, and crude glycerol electro-oxidation. Those are the ones most likely to stay useful even when the feed is messy and the power arrives as daylight-only DC.
