Here is a curated list of small-scale electrochemical reactors that meet your criteria. These systems are selected for their simplicity, tolerance to impure feedstocks, suitability for intermittent solar power, and ability to produce high-value outputs.
### 1. Undivided Chlor-Alkali Cell (Sodium Hypochlorite Generator)
This is arguably the most robust and commercially viable small-scale electrochemical reactor you can build. It converts basic saltwater into bleach (sodium hypochlorite), a critical chemical for water sanitation and surface disinfection.
 * **Mechanism:** An electric current is passed through a brine solution. At the anode, chloride ions form chlorine gas. At the cathode, water splits into hydrogen gas and hydroxide ions. In an *undivided* cell, the chlorine and hydroxide immediately mix to form sodium hypochlorite (NaOCl).
 * **Source Materials & Impurity Resilience:** Requires only water and salt (NaCl). It is highly resilient to impurities; you can run this on raw seawater, brackish water, or rock salt. The presence of calcium or magnesium in hard water will cause some scaling on the cathode, but this is easily reversed by occasionally swapping the polarity or doing a mild acid wash.
 * **Solar Integration:** Excellent. The reaction scales linearly with available current. During the night, you can drop the power to a tiny idle current (just enough to prevent the electrodes from acting as a galvanic cell and degrading).
 * **Output & Market Value:** Liquid bleach degrades quickly over months, making *local, on-demand* production highly valuable. You can sell it to local farms, municipal water treatment centers, or commercial cleaning services.
 * **Build Complexity:** Very low. Requires a simple PVC or acrylic tank, a power supply, a Titanium/Mixed Metal Oxide (MMO) anode (crucial for longevity), and a stainless steel or titanium cathode.
### 2. Electrowinning Cell for E-Waste & Scrap Recovery
Electrowinning is the process of recovering pure metals from a solution. By leaching electronic waste (PCBs, old wires) or low-grade scrap with mild acids, you can use electrochemistry to extract high-purity metals.
 * **Mechanism:** A metal-rich leachate solution is placed in a bath. When current is applied, positive metal ions (like Cu^{2+} or Ag^+) migrate to the cathode and plate out as solid, high-purity metal.
 * **Source Materials & Impurity Resilience:** The entire purpose of this reactor is to handle impurities. Because different metals reduce at different specific voltages, you can strictly control the voltage to plate out *only* the high-value metal (e.g., copper), leaving impurities (like iron or zinc) harmlessly dissolved in the solution.
 * **Solar Integration:** Ideal. Slower, low-amperage plating (which happens during cloudy periods or dawn/dusk) actually produces a denser, higher-purity metal crystal structure. A low idle current at night prevents the acidic bath from re-dissolving the plated metal.
 * **Output & Market Value:** Extremely high. You are turning scrap materials into 99.9% pure copper, silver, or gold, which can be sold at spot market prices to foundries or jewelers.
 * **Build Complexity:** Moderate. Requires an acid-resistant tank (HDPE), a lead or MMO anode, and a stainless steel starter cathode (from which you peel the plated metal). You also need a decent solar charge controller to regulate voltage accurately.
### 3. Electrocoagulation (EC) Reactor
Instead of producing a chemical product, an EC reactor produces a high-value *service*: turning heavily contaminated wastewater into usable, clean water.
 * **Mechanism:** This reactor uses sacrificial anodes (usually cheap iron or aluminum). As current passes through, the anode dissolves, releasing metal ions into the water. These ions neutralize the electrical charges of suspended pollutants, heavy metals, and oils, causing them to clump together (coagulate) and float to the surface or sink for easy removal.
 * **Source Materials & Impurity Resilience:** Designed explicitly for extreme impurities. It can handle agricultural runoff, textile dyes, mining tailings, or oily bilge water. The "fuel" is cheap scrap iron or aluminum plates.
 * **Solar Integration:** Highly adaptable. The rate of water treatment simply scales with the sun. It can be completely shut off at night with zero negative consequences to the reactor.
 * **Output & Market Value:** Clean water and concentrated sludge. The market value comes from charging for wastewater remediation or producing agricultural water in arid regions.
 * **Build Complexity:** Low. Consists of an open tank or PVC pipe with alternating plates of scrap iron or aluminum. It requires a pump to move water through the system and a basic skimmer/filter to catch the coagulated sludge.
### 4. Electrochemical Ferrate (FeVI) Synthesizer
Ferrate is a "super-oxidant"—a highly reactive form of iron that destroys bacteria, viruses, and chemical pollutants without leaving toxic byproducts (it just turns back into harmless rust).
 * **Mechanism:** An iron anode is subjected to a high current in a strongly alkaline solution (high concentration NaOH or KOH). The iron is oxidized all the way to the +6 oxidation state, creating a purple ferrate solution.
 * **Source Materials & Impurity Resilience:** Uses scrap iron/steel and lye. It is relatively tolerant to impurities in the iron, though the lye needs to be reasonably pure.
 * **Solar Integration:** The synthesis is energy-intensive, making it a great sponge for peak mid-day solar power. It can be idled at night.
 * **Output & Market Value:** Very high, but niche. Liquid ferrate is highly unstable and degrades within hours or days. Therefore, it *cannot* be shipped effectively. If you build an on-site solar ferrate reactor for an industrial client or municipal water facility, you hold a monopoly on supplying them with one of the most powerful green water-treatment chemicals in the world.
 * **Build Complexity:** Moderate. Requires safe handling of highly caustic, concentrated lye solutions and proper venting for the hydrogen gas produced at the cathode.
### System Design Notes for Solar Integration
To successfully run these on solar power with day/night cycles:
 * **Avoid complete shutdowns:** In cells like the Chlor-Alkali or Electrowinning baths, a total power loss can allow the harsh chemical bath to attack the electrodes or dissolve your produced metals.
 * **Implement a "Keep-Alive" circuit:** Use a small battery bank and a charge controller programmed to supply a continuous "trickle" polarization current (often just a few milliamps) during the night. This maintains the cathodic/anodic protection of your electrodes without draining significant power.
